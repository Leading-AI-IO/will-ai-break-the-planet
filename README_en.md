## 📖 Overview

In 2026, AI became the central topic of society. The subject discussed most fiercely is "jobs" — AI will take our work, new graduates won't find jobs, the white-collar middle will collapse. Precisely because these are near, visible, personal threats, public opinion and the media react at full force.

Meanwhile, there is a question almost no one is counting the stakes of. **As the world's AI companies, capital, and governments pour tens of trillions of yen into AI infrastructure, exactly how much CO2 will be emitted, and what will it ultimately bring to the planet, and to humanity?**

This book is not doomsaying. On the contrary, it begins by taking seriously the most authoritative official view on this issue — the IEA's optimistic forecast that "data-center CO2 is only about 1% of global emissions and will peak out around 2030." But what this book questions is not "quantity." **It is "direction" and "time."**

That peak-out rests on the premise that SMRs, new nuclear, the grid, and renewables all "arrive on schedule." That is not a forecast — it is a bet. And the time horizon on which that bet is said to "arrive in time" overlaps with the horizon on which the risk of the Earth crossing an irreversible tipping point rises sharply.

This is not a judgment. This book is an open-source work whose purpose is to connect, with a single line, four conversations that have until now been spoken of separately — **AI infrastructure investment, climate irreversibility, security consequences, and human cognitive bias — and to describe them as one structure: the "collision of timelines."**
